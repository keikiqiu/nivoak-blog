<p>You notice it every time: that first sip of bourbon is perfect — warm, complex, exactly right. But by the time you're halfway through, something's off. The flavor is thin, the finish is short, and it doesn't taste like the same whiskey anymore. The culprit? Ice melting into your drink.</p>

<p>It's not your imagination, and it's not the whiskey's fault. The change in taste over time has a specific explanation — and a practical fix.</p>

<h2>The Science of Dilution</h2>
<p>Whiskey's flavor comes from compounds called congeners — esters, aldehydes, and phenols that create the notes of vanilla, caramel, oak, and spice. When ice melts, it doesn't just add water — it changes the concentration of these compounds. The balance shifts. The flavors you loved at the first sip become muted or disappear entirely.</p>

<p>This is why experienced whiskey drinkers often prefer their whiskey neat or with just a drop of water. They want the full profile the distiller intended.</p>

<h2>The Temperature Factor</h2>
<p>Temperature also affects taste. When whiskey is very cold, your taste buds are less sensitive to sweetness and bitterness. Whiskey stones provide a middle ground: enough chill to be pleasant, not so much that it masks flavor, and zero dilution to mess with the balance. <h2>The Consistent Sip Test</h2>
<p>Try this experiment at home: pour two glasses of the same whiskey. Add ice to one and whiskey stones to the other. Take a sip from each glass every five minutes. After 20 minutes, compare them. The ice glass will be noticeably thinner. The stone glass will taste about the same as it did at minute one.</p>

<h2>What You Can Do About It</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Switch to whiskey stones</strong> — no dilution, consistent flavor from first sip to last</li>
<li><strong>Use a single large ice mold</strong> — less surface area means slower melting</li>
<li><strong>Accept the dilution</strong> — some people genuinely prefer the way whiskey opens up with a bit of water</li>
</ul>
